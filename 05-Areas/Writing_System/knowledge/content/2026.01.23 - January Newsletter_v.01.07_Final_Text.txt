Precious Metals Sector (Dynamics) 

The rally in gold and silver shows no signs of abating, despite elevated short-term overbought technical conditions. At the time of writing, TradingEconomics.com has gold at US$ 4987.7/t.oz and silver at US$102.95/t.oz).

Geopolitical Drivers: President Trump's escalating tariff threats (particularly regarding Greenland and trade imbalances with Europe) have reignited safe-haven flows. The threat of a 25% tariff on European goods by June, in addition to 10% immediate duties, is a material risk factor that supports defensive asset positioning. Central banks worldwide continue accumulating gold reserves, with China's January 2026 reclassification of silver as a "strategic dual-use metal" adding supply-side friction.

Silver's Structural Deficit: GlobalData's latest forecast, revised upward just this week, projects silver reaching $175-$220 by year-end 2026, up from previous targets of $85-$100. The rationale is compelling: (1) Industrial demand from AI data centres, solar, and electric vehicles is accelerating faster than mine supply can accommodate (silver is largely a byproduct of lead/zinc mining), (2) Investment demand is surging as investors recognise the metal's dual monetary/industrial role, and (3) East-West price arbitrage has emerged, with physical silver trading at 80%+ premiums in Shanghai and even higher in Japan, draining Western vaults. Any sustained break above $105-$110 could well trigger a parabolic phase.

Gold Momentum: With gold now approaching $5,000/oz, the previous forecasts from major banks are looking conservative. They were forecasting $5,000-$6,000 by year-end 2026. It does seem that precious metals are in the early-to-middle stages of a bull cycle driven by real yield considerations (real rates remain negative) and geopolitical uncertainty. A 30-45% rise from current levels to $6,100-$6,700 (GlobalData's year-end 2026 estimate) is quite conceivable, if political & trade tensions escalate further.

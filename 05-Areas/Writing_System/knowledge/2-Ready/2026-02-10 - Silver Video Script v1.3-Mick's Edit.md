## SECTION 1: THE PHYSICAL SILVER SHORTAGE (1:00-4:45)

**[1:00]**

Let me start with the data, then we'll discuss what it means.

**[VISUAL: Title card - "Section 1: The Physical Shortage"]**

**[1:10] Supply Deficits**

According to the Silver Institute - the industry body that tracks global silver supply and demand - the silver market has been running a supply deficit for five consecutive years from 2021 to 2025. The cumulative shortfall over that period: 820 million ounces.

**[VISUAL: Bar chart showing annual deficits 2021-2025]**

2026 is projected to be the sixth consecutive year of deficit. This isn't a temporary blip - this is a structural imbalance between supply and demand.

And whilst that deficit was already creating stress in physical markets, China's January 1st export restrictions have added acute pressure to an already tight supply situation.

**[1:40] The COMEX Delivery Crisis**

Now, here's where things get interesting. In mid-January 2026 - right around the time silver was spiking towards its all-time high - 33.45 million ounces of physical silver were withdrawn from COMEX registered inventory in just seven days.

**[VISUAL: COMEX inventory chart highlighting the January withdrawal]**

That's 26% of the entire registered inventory, gone in one week. This is one of the largest delivery events in COMEX history.

Registered inventory - that's the silver actually available for delivery against futures contracts - has collapsed from around 250 million ounces in 2020 to approximately 82 million ounces today. That's a 75% decline in six years.

**[2:20] Physical Dealer Reality**

What does this look like in the real world, where actual investors buy actual metal?

**[VISUAL: Dealer website screenshots showing availability and premiums]**

I checked APMEX, JM Bullion, and SD Bullion - three of the largest physical silver dealers - on February 9th, 2026. Here's what I found:

American Silver Eagles: approximately 18-19% premium over COMEX spot. So if spot is $81, you're paying $96-97 per ounce.

Generic silver rounds: roughly 5-7% premium over spot.

Delivery times: 2-3 weeks, versus the normal 2-3 days.

Stock status: Limited availability on popular government-minted coins like Silver Britannias.

**[VISUAL: Table comparing current premiums to historical norms]**

Now, let's put those premiums in context. In a normal market - say, 2015 to 2019 - generic rounds typically carried a 2-3% premium over spot, and American Silver Eagles ran about 5-6% over spot.

During the COVID panic in 2020, when mints shut down and supply chains froze, premiums spiked to 30-50%. We saw a similar surge during the Reddit WallStreetBets silver squeeze attempt in 2021.

**[3:20]**

Today's premiums for generic rounds have actually normalised somewhat - that 5-7% premium is elevated but not crisis-level. However, American Silver Eagles are still running triple their normal premium. This suggests ongoing supply constraints for government-minted coins, even as generic rounds become more available.

**[3:40] Industrial vs Investment Demand**

Where's the demand coming from?

We all know that silver has a dual nature. It's both an investment asset and an industrial metal - and that's crucial to understanding the current dynamics.

**[VISUAL: Pie chart showing silver demand breakdown - industrial vs investment]**

On the industrial side, silver is critical for solar panels (the largest industrial use), electronics manufacturing, electric vehicles, 5G infrastructure, and medical applications. These are largely inelastic demands - if you're building a solar panel, you need silver. You can't substitute it easily, and you can't wait for a better price.

On the investment side, we've seen surging demand in 2025 amid economic uncertainty, both from retail investors buying physical metal and institutional flows into silver ETFs.

The result: continuing structural deficit. Supply growth has remained relatively flat, whilst demand from both industrial and investment sources has surged.

**[4:15]**

With that context in mind - a five-year cumulative deficit of 820 million ounces, collapsing COMEX inventories, elevated premiums, delivery delays, and now China restricting exports - let's now turn to how the paper silver market actually works. Because that's where the disconnect becomes truly stark.

## SECTION 2: COMEX PAPER SILVER EXPLAINED (4:45-8:15)

**[4:45]**

**[VISUAL: Title card - "Section 2: COMEX Paper Silver"]**

If you're new to commodities markets, the term "paper silver" can sound a bit abstract. So let me explain the mechanics.

**[5:00] What is COMEX?**

COMEX stands for Commodity Exchange Inc., now part of the CME Group. It's the primary western exchange where silver futures contracts are traded.

A standard COMEX silver futures contract represents 5,000 troy ounces of silver. These contracts have delivery months - March, May, July, September, and December.

**[VISUAL: Animated diagram showing futures contract structure]**

Here's the key point: Most contracts are cash-settled, not physically delivered. Traders buy and sell these contracts to speculate on silver prices or hedge price risk. When the contract expires, they typically settle the difference in cash rather than taking delivery of actual metal.

**[5:35] Who Trades Paper Silver?**

There are four main categories of participants:

**Hedgers** - miners who want to lock in future prices for their production, or industrial users who want to lock in future costs for their silver consumption.

**Speculators** - traders betting on price movements with no intent to take delivery. They're here for profit, not metal.

**Market makers** - typically large banks that provide liquidity to the market.

**Arbitrageurs** - traders exploiting price differences between spot markets, futures markets, and physical markets.

**[VISUAL: Four icons representing each participant type]**

**[6:05] The Leverage Ratio**

Now we come to the critical issue: leverage.

As of February 3rd, 2026, total open interest on COMEX silver - that's the number of outstanding contracts - stood at 143,180 contracts.

Multiply that by 5,000 ounces per contract, and you get 715.9 million ounces of paper claims on silver.

**[VISUAL: Calculation shown on screen: 143,180 x 5,000 = 715.9M oz]**

Meanwhile, registered inventory - the physical silver actually available for delivery - stands at approximately 82 million ounces.

Divide paper claims by physical inventory: 715.9 million divided by 82 million equals a leverage ratio of 8.7 to 1.

**[VISUAL: Leverage ratio visualization - 8.7 paper claims for every 1 oz of physical]**

**[6:55]**

What does that mean in plain English? It means there are 8.7 paper claims for every ounce of physical silver available for delivery. The system relies on the assumption that most people won't demand physical delivery.

But here's where it gets more concerning. For the March 2026 delivery month specifically, open interest represents 425 to 455 million ounces. That's just one month's worth of contracts. Divide that by the 82 million ounces of registered inventory, and you get a ratio of 5.2 to 5.5 to 1.

**[VISUAL: March contracts calculation shown]**

If only 20% of March contract holders demanded physical delivery, that would be 85 to 91 million ounces. Current registered inventory: 82 million ounces. We'd have a shortfall of 3 to 9 million ounces... in other words, the Comex inventory will be wiped out!

And remember: this registered inventory sits primarily in COMEX vaults, but the pool of physical silver available globally just got smaller. China's export restrictions mean less refined silver flowing from the world's largest refining hub into Western markets.

**[7:35]**

Now, in normal times, this fractional reserve system functions perfectly well. Most traders don't want delivery - they want price exposure. Hedgers roll their contracts forward. Cash settlement keeps the system liquid.

But what happens when that assumption breaks down? What happens when a significant percentage of contract holders say, "I actually want the metal"?

**[8:00]**

We saw a glimpse of that in mid-January 2026. 33.45 million ounces withdrawn in seven days. CME responded by raising margin requirements to 60%, which triggered cascading liquidations. Silver crashed from its all-time high of $121.67 down to the $80s in a single day.

**[VISUAL: Timeline of January events]**

The market structure came under severe stress. And whilst there's been no formal force majeure declaration - no official delivery failure - the cracks are showing.

## SECTION 3: THE DISCONNECT (8:15-12:15)

**[8:15]**

**[VISUAL: Title card - "Section 3: The Disconnect"]**

So we have a physical market with structural deficits, collapsing inventories, and elevated premiums. And we have a paper market with extreme leverage ratios and periodic delivery stress.

How did we get here, and what are the historical precedents?

**[8:35] Historical Precedents**

This isn't the first time commodity markets have shown this kind of disconnect between paper prices and physical availability.

**[VISUAL: Timeline showing historical commodity disconnects]**

In 2008, during the financial crisis, gold and silver premiums spiked to 20-30% over spot. Dealers ran out of inventory despite falling paper prices. Mints rationed production. The disconnect lasted several months until supply chains normalised.

In 2020, during COVID, we saw the same pattern. COMEX silver fell to $12 per ounce, but try finding physical silver below $18-20. Premiums hit 30-50%. Mints shut down, supply chains disrupted. It took six months to normalise.

**[9:15]**

Perhaps the most instructive example is the 2022 nickel crisis on the London Metal Exchange. A short squeeze forced the exchange to halt trading and cancel trades retroactively. Physical nickel diverged from paper nickel. The lesson: **exchanges will intervene to protect market function - and certain participants - when stress becomes too severe.**

**[9:35] The Bull and Bear Cases**

There are two very different interpretations of this disconnect.

**[VISUAL: Split screen - Bulls vs Bears]**

The bearish view - or perhaps the sceptical view - argues that physical premiums are simply dealer markup, not a true shortage. COMEX provides price discovery and liquidity. Industrial users rely on COMEX for hedging. The delivery option keeps the paper price tethered to physical reality. Premiums reflect retail distribution costs, not fundamental scarcity.

**[10:05]**

The bullish view - the physical advocates' position - argues that the paper market is a fractionally reserved system that's fundamentally unsustainable. Banks can print unlimited paper contracts. Price suppression benefits industrial users and governments who want to keep inflation signals low. **The physical market is the "real" market because you can't print metal.** Premiums expanding signal that the market is rejecting the paper price. Eventually, delivery demands will force convergence.

**[10:35] The China Factor**

Now, let me add another layer to this disconnect - and this is where the situation becomes genuinely more complex than previous supply squeezes.

On January 1st, 2026, China implemented licence-based export controls on refined silver. Only 44 state-approved companies - those producing at least 80 tonnes of silver annually - can now export silver from China.

**[VISUAL: Map showing China's 60-70% refining dominance, with export flow arrows]**

Here's why this matters: China doesn't mine the majority of global silver. They account for roughly 13% of global silver mining. But they control 60-70% of global silver refining. Hundreds of smaller Chinese refiners who previously exported silver are now permanently cut from the supply chain.

**[11:05]**

China has reclassified silver as a "strategic material" - the same category as rare earths. And if you recall what happened with rare earths, this should concern you.

**[VISUAL: Timeline - Rare earths 2010 licensing -> 4,500% price spike]**

In 2010, China imposed similar licensing requirements on rare earth exports. Prices subsequently spiked 4,500%. The pattern is consistent: licence first, restrict gradually, then weaponize if geopolitically convenient.

**[11:25]**

The immediate market impact has been striking. Shanghai silver premiums are running 12% or more over London and COMEX spot prices. The global silver market, which once functioned as a relatively integrated whole, is fragmenting into three regional islands: Asia, North America, and Europe.

**[VISUAL: Map showing regional market fragmentation with price differentials]**

For COMEX and the LBMA - that's the London Bullion Market Association - this means less physical silver is available to satisfy delivery demands. The 82 million ounces in COMEX registered inventory looks even thinner when you consider that Chinese refining output is no longer flowing freely into Western markets.

**[11:55] Expert Perspectives**

Keith Neumeyer, CEO of First Majestic Silver, said in January 2026 - right before the crash - "You just can't continually run in a deficit in the physical market and not have it show up in the paper market."

He also noted: "The cracks in the paper silver system - COMEX and LBMA - are showing, with LBMA vault inventories having plunged 33% this year, and the COMEX losing 29 million ounces in a single month."

**[VISUAL: Keith Neumeyer quote on screen]**

Ted Butler, a long-time silver market analyst who sadly recently passed away, was cautiously optimistic but warned to "exercise caution, because I believe we're due a correction." Given the subsequent crash from $121 to the $80s, that proved prescient.

The Silver Institute CEO Michael DiRienzo confirmed that the structural deficit will "more than likely extend into a sixth year in 2026," highlighting the 820 million ounce cumulative deficit from 2021 to 2025.

**[12:25]**

Where does the truth lie? As far as I'm concerned, the data suggests stress in the system. Whether that stress resolves through higher prices, forced cash settlement, COMEX rule changes, or a collapse in demand is genuinely uncertain. Markets are messy, and anyone claiming certainty about how this plays out is selling you something.

What we can say with confidence is that the disconnect exists, it's widening under stress, China has now added geopolitical risk to the supply equation, and all of this creates both risks and opportunities for investors.

## SECTION 4: INVESTMENT IMPLICATIONS (12:15-14:30)

**[12:15]**

**[VISUAL: Title card - "Section 4: Investment Implications"]**

So what does all this mean if you're considering silver as part of your portfolio?

Let me break this down by investment approach.

**[12:30] Physical Silver**

If you own physical silver - coins, bars, stored in your safe or a vault - you own the actual metal. No counterparty risk. If premiums widen further, your holdings potentially increase in value beyond the spot price movement.

**[VISUAL: Pros/Cons list for physical silver]**

However, you're paying those current premiums - 5-7% for generic rounds, 18-19% for American Silver Eagles. You've got storage and security costs. Liquidity is lower than paper - you'll face bid/ask spreads and dealer fees when you sell. And if supply normalises, those premiums could collapse back to 2-3%, meaning you'd face a loss on the premium you paid - if you try and trade it 'short term'!

That said, China's export restrictions introduce a new variable. If Chinese refined silver becomes structurally less available to Western markets, premiums could remain elevated for far longer than in previous supply disruptions.

**[13:10] Paper Silver - ETFs and Futures**

Paper silver - whether through ETFs like SLV, futures contracts, or silver mining stocks - offers liquidity and easy tradability. Lower transaction costs. Exposure to silver price movements without storage hassles.

**[VISUAL: Pros/Cons list for paper silver]**

The trade-off: counterparty risk. In a true shortage scenario, you may not be able to convert your paper claims to physical metal. The paper price may not capture premium expansion. And you're subject to the manipulation concerns we've discussed - margin hikes, rule changes, potential forced cash settlement.

China's export restrictions add another dimension to this risk. If Western exchanges face persistent delivery stress because Chinese refining capacity is offline for export markets, the gap between paper and physical could widen further.

**[13:50] Mining Stocks**

Silver mining stocks offer leveraged exposure to silver prices - when silver rises, miners' profits can rise even faster. Some pay dividends. There's also production growth potential.

But you're taking on operational risk, management risk, jurisdictional risk. A mine in a politically unstable country faces different risks than a bar in your safe.

That said, careful analysis can still find opportunities in this sector (IMO), as we've been discussing in recent Inner Circle Webinars on DIY-Investors.com.

**[14:10] Risk Scenarios**

What could go wrong?

**[VISUAL: List of risk scenarios]**

Premium collapse if supply normalises. Mints ramp up production (if they're able to!), demand cools, premiums compress back to 2-3%. Physical buyers at current premiums would face losses (at least in the short term).

COMEX rule changes. History shows that margin hikes and position limits killed the 1980 and 2011 silver rallies. If volatility spikes, expect regulatory intervention that hurts momentum traders. However, note that this won't reduce the demand for physical silver for industrial use!

Paper price crashes whilst physical becomes unavailable. We saw this in 2020 - COMEX sold off, physical unobtainable. Paper investors suffer losses, physical investors are unaffected but illiquid.

China further tightens export restrictions. The 44-company whitelist could shrink further. Export quotas could be introduced. This would compound the supply stress.

China weaponizes silver exports geopolitically. Think rare earths during the 2010 Japan dispute. Silver could easily become leverage in US-China tensions.

**[14:35]**

The flip side: opportunity scenarios. Industrial demand surge from solar, EVs, 5G could push both paper and physical prices higher. Currency crisis or inflation hedge demand could trigger flight to hard assets. Investment demand awakening - another Reddit-style retail surge - could spike both markets short-term. Regional price dislocations create arbitrage opportunities for those positioned to exploit them.

**Silver-to-gold ratio** - currently around 33:1, well below the recent average of 60-70:1. This suggests silver is expensive relative to gold, which could mean silver is overvalued, gold is undervalued, or the market expects strong industrial demand for silver going forward. There is an alternative view that perhaps this is just reversion to the 'long-term mean' for the silver to gold ratio.

**China export data** - watch monthly Chinese silver export volumes. Sharp declines would confirm the licensing restrictions are binding. Stabilisation might suggest workarounds emerging.

My aim in this video isn't to tell you that silver is going to the moon or crashing to zero. What I am telling you is that there's a genuine disconnect between the physical market and the paper market, and understanding that disconnect - now compounded by geopolitical factors - is essential for making informed decisions.

## Answer

**Potential Rights to Onward Property or Proceeds (PROPPs)** are non-transferable,
contingent beneficial interests used by the Bank of England during a bank bail-in
resolution. Created at the exact moment a bank enters resolution, they serve as a
temporary placeholder for bailed-in creditors (primarily bondholders). PROPPs
legally entitle holders to eventually receive shares in the rescued bank -- or the
proceeds from the sale of those shares -- once final valuations are completed and
individual creditor positions are confirmed.

### How PROPPs Differ from Certificates of Entitlement (CEs)

PROPPs serve as an alternative to Certificates of Entitlement, adding optionality
to the BoE's resolution toolkit rather than fully replacing CEs. The primary
difference lies in transferability:

**Transferability and Liquidity:** Under the older CE regime, the interim
instruments could be traded, giving sophisticated bondholders price discovery and
an option to exit positions before final shares were allocated. PROPPs are
strictly non-transferable -- bailed-in bondholders cannot sell or trade their
interim claims. While viewed as a liquidity disadvantage by some fixed-income
professionals, the BoE describes creation of PROPPs as "operationally more
straightforward" than issuing CEs.

**Operational Simplicity:** PROPPs reduce the operational and legal complexity of
the bail-in process. PROPP beneficiaries are divided into classes corresponding to
the category of liabilities they held in the failed firm, ranked as they would
have been in a standard insolvency.

### The Problem They Solve for US-Based Bondholders

PROPPs directly address a critical vulnerability in cross-border crisis management
exposed by the 2023 banking crises (SVB UK and Credit Suisse).

US securities law applies to any US-based bondholder regardless of where the
failing bank is headquartered. Under the previous CE framework, issuing tradable
CEs to US-based investors risked triggering mandatory registration under the US
Securities Act -- a process taking months, completely incompatible with the tight
timeline of a "resolution weekend."

Because PROPPs are non-transferable contingent beneficial interests rather than
registered, tradable securities, they sidestep this registration requirement
entirely. To guarantee this, the BoE secured a No-Action Letter from the SEC,
formally confirming that creating PROPPs for US investors does not require
registration under US securities law -- removing a major practical obstacle to
resolving a multinational bank quickly.
